01 — The problem

Carriers bill the greater of dead weight and cubic weight. Cubic weight is pure geometry.

At Australia's 250–333 kg per cubic metre conversion, every centimetre of dimensional error is multiplied into the invoice — on every shipment, on every lane, forever. For standard cartons the numbers are known. For irregular freight, they're guessed.

per 10 cm of height error

Mis-estimate one dimension by a hand's width and chargeable weight moves ~24 kg. On a lane running 50–80 units a week, that compounds into a material annual number.

30–40%

quote-to-invoice variance

Reported by practitioners on poorly specified freight: depot re-measures, reclassification, accessorial surprises. The root cause is bad data at the moment of quoting.

Mispricing

Guessed dims → wrong chargeable weight on cubic freight, wrong trailer and missed permits on oversized freight. The error is amplified 250–333× per cubic metre.

Over-trucking

No trustworthy fit view → operators book a second truck "to be safe." Load-optimisation vendors cite 6–15% utilisation gains on standard freight; eye-balled irregular freight has far more slack.

Disputes

Every re-measure and re-bill erodes margin and trust. A verified measurement with an audit trail turns each argument into a settled fact.

02 — The gap

Freight software knows everything about the shipment — except the shipment.

The commercial data layers are mature. The physical ones barely exist. That bottom band is the entire wedge.

Customer & ordersERP / TMS · good
Carrier ratesTMS / broker · good
Tracking & invoicescarrier APIs · adequate
Dimensions / cubic profileyard guess · poor
Load fit / geometry"will it fit?" · very poor
Restraint / DG / oversizePDF rules · inconsistent

Every existing load-planning tool assumes you already know the item's dimensions and will type them in — which is exactly the step that fails for irregular freight. Stereon attacks the bottleneck itself: capturing physical truth at source.
